# Day 4 findings — customer-level drivers of the Sydney revenue drop

Generated by running `sql/04_customer_analysis.sql` against the same loaded
dataset as Day 3. Same convention: July 2026 excluded (partial month).

**Regenerated after the Day 5 data-generation patch.** The original Day 4
run flagged a real data-generation bug: orders were assigned to customers
independent of `signup_date`, which broke the new-vs-returning view and
made acquisition untestable. That bug is now fixed (see `nb_config.py` and
`04_generate_orders.ipynb`), so this version can finally answer the
original question in full.

Day 3 established revenue fell ~48% in May 2026, isolated to Sydney.
Revenue decomposes as `active customers x orders per customer x average
order value`. This decomposes that number across all three terms, plus
acquisition.

## 1. Active customer count fell sharply — the primary driver

| Month    | Active customers | Order count | Revenue    |
|----------|------------------:|------------:|-----------:|
| 2026-03  | 314               | 402         | $205,329   |
| 2026-04  | 313               | 397         | $180,946   |
| 2026-05  | **170**           | 187         | $93,946    |
| 2026-06  | **198**           | 233         | $112,447   |

Active customers fell 313 -> 170 (-45.7%) from April to May, closely
tracking the 48% revenue drop. Most of the magnitude is explained by fewer
distinct Sydney customers placing any order at all, not by the same
customers spending less per visit.

## 2. New vs returning customers — acquisition is a real but secondary factor

| Month    | New customers | Returning customers | Total active |
|----------|--------------:|---------------------:|-------------:|
| 2026-03  | 23            | 291                  | 314          |
| 2026-04  | 33            | 280                  | 313          |
| 2026-05  | 13            | 157                  | 170          |
| 2026-06  | 32            | 166                  | 198          |

Both segments dropped in May, but not proportionally the same:

- **Returning customers**: 280 -> 157, a loss of 123 customers (-43.9%)
- **New customers**: 33 -> 13, a loss of 20 customers (-60.6% relatively,
  but a much smaller absolute contribution)

In absolute terms, the collapse in *returning* customers accounts for
roughly 6x more of the customer-count decline than the drop in new
customers (123 vs 20). Acquisition softened too — this isn't a case of
"acquisition is fine" — but it is not the dominant cause.

## 3. Repeat purchase rate also fell — a secondary contributor on top of #1

| Month    | Active customers | Repeat customers | Repeat rate |
|----------|------------------:|------------------:|------------:|
| 2026-03  | 314               | 71                | 22.6%       |
| 2026-04  | 313               | 79                | 25.2%       |
| 2026-05  | 170               | 16                | 9.4%        |
| 2026-06  | 198               | 31                | 15.7%       |

Of the customers who *did* stay active, a smaller share placed a second
order within the month (25.2% -> 9.4%). This compounds the active-customer
decline rather than causing it independently — fewer people showed up,
and the ones who did show up were less likely to come back a second time
in the same month.

## 4. Retention rate confirms it directly

| Month    | Retention rate (vs prior month) | Churn rate |
|----------|----------------------------------:|-----------:|
| 2026-03  | 39.5%                             | 60.5%      |
| 2026-04  | 36.9%                             | 63.1%      |
| 2026-05  | **21.1%**                         | **78.9%**  |
| 2026-06  | 23.5%                             | 76.5%      |

Month-to-month retention nearly halves right at the anomaly boundary
(36.9% -> 21.1%) and stays depressed into June. This is the clearest
single signal in the dataset: existing Sydney customers stopped coming
back at close to twice their normal rate, starting exactly in May.

## 5. Average order value stayed flat — ruled out as a cause

| Month    | Avg order value |
|----------|-----------------:|
| 2026-03  | $510.77          |
| 2026-04  | $455.78          |
| 2026-05  | $502.38          |
| 2026-06  | $482.60          |

AOV moves within a normal band across the entire window, including the
drop months. Customers who do order are spending roughly the same as
before. This rules out "basket size shrank" or "customers switched to
cheaper items" as explanations.

## Conclusion: acquisition, retention, frequency, or AOV?

Ranking the four candidate explanations, now that acquisition is testable:

1. **Primarily a retention decline.** Returning-customer count fell by 123
   between April and May, month-over-month retention nearly halved
   (36.9% -> 21.1%), and repeat purchase rate among active customers
   collapsed (25.2% -> 9.4%). These three views agree with each other and
   account for most of the revenue drop's magnitude.
2. **Secondarily, an acquisition decline.** New customer count also fell
   (33 -> 13), a real and directionally consistent signal, but roughly
   6x smaller in absolute customer-count terms than the retention effect.
3. **Not AOV.** Flat throughout, ruled out.

The honest summary: *"Sydney's revenue drop is driven mainly by existing
customers churning at close to twice their normal rate starting in May,
with a smaller but real contribution from fewer new customers being
acquired over the same period. Average spend per order was unaffected."*

This still doesn't answer *why* Sydney customers churned — SQL describes
the "what" and "how much," not the "why." That gap is what the ML anomaly
model and, later, the LLM explanation layer need to close.
